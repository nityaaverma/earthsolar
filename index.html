Introduction to Solar Panels

Solar panels convert sunlight into electricity using photovoltaic (PV) cells. When photons strike a semiconductor material—typically silicon—they knock electrons loose, creating an electric current that can power homes, schools, and devices.

Most panels you see today are either monocrystalline (made from a single crystal of silicon, offering high efficiency) or polycrystalline (made from many silicon fragments, generally more affordable). Both types produce clean, renewable energy with no fuel costs and no direct emissions during operation.

Solar energy matters because it reduces dependence on fossil fuels, lowers electricity bills over time, and helps fight climate change. As technology improves and costs continue to fall, solar power is becoming one of the fastest-growing sources of electricity worldwide.

My Project: A Smart and Efficient Solar Panel

This project aims to design and build a solar panel system that is not only efficient at capturing sunlight, but also smart—capable of monitoring its own performance and adapting to changing conditions.

Key goals include tracking real-time power output, automatically adjusting the panel angle to follow the sun for maximum exposure, and using sensors to detect shading or faults early. By combining hardware (solar cells, motors, microcontrollers) with simple software, the system can optimize energy harvest throughout the day.

The vision is a panel that anyone can understand and maintain: clear data on how much energy is being produced, alerts when something needs attention, and design choices that prioritize both efficiency and affordability.

Description

EarthSolar is a personal learning and engineering project focused on renewable energy. It brings together research on how solar panels work, hands-on experimentation with hardware, and the goal of making clean energy technology more accessible and intelligent.

Through this work, I hope to demonstrate that students and hobbyists can contribute meaningfully to the clean-energy transition—not just by using solar panels, but by improving how they perform in the real world.

This site will be updated as the project progresses with new findings, design changes, and results. Check back for updates, or visit the GitHub repository to follow along with the source code and documentation.
